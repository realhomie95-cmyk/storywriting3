Dr. Tanaka didn't speak for the first ten minutes. She moved along the row with the precision of someone who'd spent thirty years reading paintings the way doctors read x-rays. She'd pause. Lean close. Tilt her head. Move on.

When she reached the smallest canvas — the abstract composition, faded greens and blacks and a violent red slash across the center — she stopped.

I didn't respond.

But I didn't delete it either.

Because here's what I know about my father: he wasn't punishing Garrett. He wasn't rewarding me. He was doing what he always did — putting the right thing in the hands of the person who would take care of it.

The paintings needed someone who would look.

He knew I would.

He was right.
