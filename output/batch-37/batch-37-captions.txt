My name is Priya Anand. I'm thirty-seven years old. I co-founded Ridgeline AI six years ago in a one-bedroom apartment in Boulder, Colorado, with a secondhand GPU cluster and a whiteboard covered in math that nobody but me understood.

I built the algorithm. I wrote the first 140,000 lines of code. I trained the model that won us our seed round, our Series A, our first enterprise contract, and every technical breakthrough that followed.

And this morning, my co-founder — the man I brought in to handle fundraising because I hated pitch meetings — tried to fire me.

Marcus Hale. Forty-three. Ex-McKinsey. The kind of man who wears a navy blazer to a board meeting and calls it "casual." He joined Ridgeline in year two when I realized I needed someone who could talk to VCs without glazing over at the word "revenue." I gave him the CEO title because I didn't want it. I wanted to build.

For four years, it worked.

Then we raised our Series C. Forty-two million. And Marcus decided that raising money made him the architect. He started cutting R&D budgets to pad gross margins for the next fundraise. He sidelined three of my best engineers — reassigned them to "growth" projects that had nothing to do with the core technology. He hired a VP of Product who'd never shipped software and gave her authority over my technical roadmap.

I pushed back. Privately, at first. Then in board meetings. Then in emails that got longer and more pointed with each passing month.

Marcus stopped responding to my emails in March.

In April, he moved the engineering stand-up to a time slot I'd told him I couldn't attend — 6 AM, three days a week. My daughter has seizures. I can't leave her with anyone before seven.

In May, he told the board I was "disengaged."

In June, he told them I'd "lost the technical vision."

I heard about both statements secondhand. From our corporate secretary, who still answers my calls.

And last Friday, Marcus sent a calendar invite: "Emergency Board Session — Governance Vote." Monday 9 AM. The agenda line read: "Motion to remove C-suite member for cause (performance)."

He didn't say my name. He didn't have to.

The board is three people. All Marcus's appointees — brought in during the Series C as a condition of the lead investor. Two of them have never opened our product. The third thinks machine learning is "like ChatGPT but for spreadsheets."

The vote was going to be 3-0. Marcus told the corporate secretary to have separation paperwork ready by noon. My equity vesting would be frozen. My access revoked. My name stripped from the company I built in a single Monday morning.

Eleven minutes. That's what he estimated.

I logged in to the video call at 8:59 AM from my home office. Gray t-shirt. Braid over one shoulder. Wire-rimmed glasses.

Marcus was standing at the head of the boardroom table in LoDo. Navy blazer. Mock-neck underneath. Hair styled. He looked like a man about to give a TED talk, not fire his co-founder.

The three board members sat with tablets and water glasses. Two men, one woman. None of them made eye contact with my video feed.

"Good morning," Marcus said. "Let's begin. I'll keep this brief."

"Before you do," I said.

He paused. His hand was still raised mid-gesture.

"I'd like to ask the corporate secretary to read something aloud," I said.

Marcus frowned. "Priya, this isn't —"

"Article 9, Section 3 of the original operating agreement," I said. "The one filed with the Colorado Secretary of State in 2020."

The corporate secretary — a junior attorney named Beth who Marcus clearly hadn't briefed — looked at her binder. Then at Marcus. Then back at me.

"Go ahead, Beth," I said.

Marcus's jaw tightened.

Beth flipped pages. Found the section. Cleared her throat.

"Article 9, Section 3. Founder Super-Voting Provisions. Quote: 'Each share held by a Founding Member shall carry ten votes on all matters of corporate governance, including but not limited to removal of officers, amendment of operating agreements, and dissolution of roles. This provision may not be amended without unanimous consent of all Founding Members.' End quote."

The room went very quiet.

Marcus hadn't amended it. He'd never asked for my consent — because he'd assumed I'd never fight. He'd assumed the quiet woman who let him take the CEO title would let him take everything else too.

But I built this company. Line by line. Function by function. And I read every single document before I signed it.

"Thank you, Beth," I said. "Now — I'd like to make a motion."

Marcus's face changed. The confidence drained. The color followed.

"I move to remove Marcus Hale as CEO of Ridgeline AI, effective immediately. My super-voting shares represent sixty-two percent of governance authority. I call the vote."

The board members looked at each other. Then at Marcus. Then at the operating agreement still open in Beth's binder.

Eleven minutes.

That's how long the whole thing was supposed to take.

I saw the truck before I heard the knock. White pickup. Corporate logo on the door. Parked past my cattle guard like it belonged there.

My name is Loretta Mae Hutchins. I'm seventy-two years old. I've ranched this land — six hundred and forty acres fourteen miles south of Midland, Texas — since I was nineteen. My grandfather filed the original homestead in 1921. My father expanded it in the fifties. My husband Bill mapped every inch of the subsurface geology before he died four years ago.

Three generations of Hutchins women and men have lived and worked and been buried on this dirt. We've survived droughts and dust storms and oil busts and cattle disease and every single thing the Permian Basin can throw at a family that refuses to leave.

But this was new.

The man who stepped out of the white truck was young — thirty-eight, maybe. Buzz cut. Pressed khakis that hadn't seen a day of honest work. Company polo stretched tight across his chest. He carried a clipboard the way a preacher carries a Bible — like it gave him authority he hadn't earned.

His name, he told me when he reached the porch steps, was Dale Sutter. He worked for Permian Pipeline Corporation. And he was here to inform me — not ask, inform — that a two-hundred-foot easement corridor would be running through the southwest quarter of my property for a new crude oil transmission line.

"Ma'am, this is a courtesy visit," he said. He held up the clipboard like a shield. "The route has already been surveyed. If you sign the voluntary agreement today, the company will compensate you at fair market rates. If you don't —"

"If I don't?"

He shifted his weight. Couldn't quite meet my eyes.

"If you don't, ma'am, the company will proceed with eminent domain proceedings through the county."

I stood in my doorway. Straw hat shading my eyes. Pearl-snap shirt. Dusty Wranglers. Arms crossed.

Behind Dale, the late afternoon sun turned the mesquite trees into black silhouettes against a sky so wide it makes city people feel small. The windmill creaked. A red-tailed hawk circled the stock tank.

This land.

Bill used to say this land remembers everything. Every fence post set. Every well drilled. Every aquifer recharged by a hundred years of careful stewardship.

Bill was a hydrogeologist. PhD from Texas Tech, 1978. Worked for the state water board for twenty years before he retired and spent the rest of his life studying what was under our feet. Not oil. Water.

The Ogallala Aquifer.

Most people think of it as that big underground reservoir somewhere up north — Kansas, Nebraska. But the Ogallala has recharge zones all the way down into West Texas. And one of those zones — a critical one, a federally mapped one — runs directly beneath my southwest quarter.

Bill knew this. Bill mapped it. Bill filed papers I didn't fully understand at the time but kept in his desk because he told me to.

"They'll come," he said. Six months before he died. Sitting at that same desk with his reading glasses slipping down his nose and a hand-drawn map spread across the blotter. "Sooner or later, they'll come for this corridor. And when they do — show them."

He put a sticky note on the map.

They'll come. Show them.

I didn't look at the map for four years. I just ranched. Fed cattle. Fixed fence. Paid taxes. Kept going because that's what Hutchins women do.

But when Dale Sutter stepped onto my porch with his clipboard and his polite threats, I felt something shift inside me. Not fear. Not anger.

Recognition.

Bill knew.

"Mr. Sutter," I said. "You can tell your company I won't be signing anything today."

His face tightened. "Ma'am, I'd strongly recommend you consult with an attorney before —"

"I don't need an attorney," I said. "I need you off my porch."

He opened his mouth.

"And you can tell Permian Pipeline that if they want to discuss this further, they can do it at the county hearing next Wednesday." I took one step forward. He took one step back. "I'll be there. With documents your survey team apparently missed."

Dale stood there for a moment. Clipboard lowered. He looked at me — really looked — and I think for the first time in his career, the man on the other side of the cattle guard wasn't afraid.

I wasn't afraid.

I was ready.

Because sitting in Bill's desk right now — in the bottom left drawer, under his field notebooks and his USGS quadrangle maps — is a hand-drawn aquifer chart. And beneath it, a deed. Filed in 1957 by my father-in-law, Harlan Hutchins.

A water rights deed.

And unless Permian Pipeline has found a way around the Safe Drinking Water Act, that deed means their two-hundred-foot corridor doesn't cross my land.

It crosses a federally protected recharge zone.

And the woman who owns the water rights is standing on this porch.

I read the list three times before my hands started shaking.

Not because I was angry. Because the fourth title on the list — a picture book called When the Sky Feels Heavy — is the reason my daughter is still alive.

My name is Oliver Nash. I'm thirty-eight years old. I'm a children's librarian at the Naperville Public Library in Naperville, Illinois. I shelve books. I run story time on Tuesday mornings. I help eight-year-olds find graphic novels and fourteen-year-olds find themselves in characters who feel as lost as they do.

I'm also a widower. My wife Caitlin died three years ago. Brain aneurysm. She was thirty-four. One morning she was making pancakes. By noon she was gone.

Our daughter Wren was five.

Wren didn't speak for four months after Caitlin died. Not a word. Not to me. Not to her teachers. Not to the therapist I drove her to every Wednesday at a clinic forty minutes away because it was the only practice with an opening.

The therapist — Dr. Amara Hughes — used books. Specific books. She'd read them to Wren in session and afterward Wren would draw. Not talk. Draw. Pictures of clouds and houses and a woman with long hair who was always waving goodbye from a window.

The book that broke through was When the Sky Feels Heavy. It's a picture book about a bear cub whose mother hibernates and doesn't wake up. The cub carries the sky on his shoulders because he thinks that's what strong bears do. Until a fox tells him: "You don't have to hold the sky. You can just stand under it."

Wren drew a picture of herself standing under the sky. Not holding it.

That was the day she spoke again.

She said: "Daddy, can we get a fox?"

We didn't get a fox. But we got a cat named Clover and a life that slowly, painfully, week by week, started to feel like something worth waking up for.

When the Sky Feels Heavy saved my daughter. I believe that with everything I have.

And now Cheryl Davenport wants to ban it.

Cheryl is the PTA president at Elmwood Elementary — the school Wren attends. Forty-five. Highlighted blond hair. Cream blouse. Statement necklace. The kind of woman who arrives fifteen minutes early to every meeting with printed agendas and color-coded folders and an energy that suggests she's running for office even when she's just running a bake sale.

Two weeks ago, Cheryl circulated a petition to ban twelve books from the school library. The petition was titled "Protecting Our Children's Innocence" and it listed titles that she claimed contained "age-inappropriate themes including death, divorce, mental illness, and identity confusion."

Twelve books. Most of them picture books. Several of them award winners.

Number four on the list: When the Sky Feels Heavy.

The petition got forty-three signatures in five days. Most parents signed without reading the list. I know this because I asked three of them what was on it and none could name more than two titles.

The vote was scheduled for Thursday's PTA meeting. Majority rules. Simple up-or-down.

I could have fought it publicly. I could have stood up at the meeting and told them what that book meant to my daughter. I could have made them feel guilty.

But I'm a librarian. I don't fight with emotion. I fight with policy.

So I went to the district office on Monday morning. I requested two documents.

The first: the school district's Materials Review Policy. A fourteen-page document adopted in 2018 that establishes the procedure for challenging library materials. It requires a formal review committee, a minimum thirty-day evaluation period, and a written rationale tied to specific educational standards. No book can be removed by majority vote at a PTA meeting. The procedure is clear. The vote Cheryl scheduled is invalid.

The second document I requested was from the school library's donation records.

Every book in the Elmwood Elementary library has a source record. Most are purchased through the annual budget. Some are donated. Donated books have a card in the system — donor name, date, sometimes a dedication.

I looked up When the Sky Feels Heavy.

Donated: September 2023.

Donor: Cheryl Davenport.

Dedication: "For my son, Noah. Because some feelings are too big for words."

I sat at the district office desk and read that card three times.

Cheryl Davenport donated the book she's now trying to ban. Three years ago. For her own son. Who was — I later learned — in grief counseling after Cheryl's mother passed unexpectedly.

She knows what this book does. She knows exactly what it means.

And something happened between then and now — pressure from other parents, a viral post about "woke books," a school board election coming in November — that made her put it on a list.

I didn't call her. I didn't confront her.

I just put both documents in a manila folder. And I went to the Thursday meeting.

I sat in the middle row. Folding chair. Corduroy jacket. Folder on my lap.

Cheryl stood at the podium with her petition raised in one hand and her projected slide behind her.

"The vote tonight is simple," she said. "We're protecting our children from material that causes confusion and distress."

Forty parents nodded.

I waited.

Because when I stand up — and I will stand up — I'm not going to humiliate her.

I'm going to save twelve books.

And I'm going to tell her — privately, afterward — that one of those books saved my daughter's life.

And that she's the one who put it on the shelf.
