Every Sunday for thirty years, the same two figures stood twenty feet apart in the same backyard in Polish Hill.

The old man in the faded Steelers sweatshirt would wind up slow — slower every year, his shoulder dropping earlier, his follow-through shorter — and throw. The younger one, stocky like his father, would catch it in the same beat-up mitt he had since Little League and throw it back. No words. No score. Just the sound of leather on leather and the rhythm of two men who had forgotten how to talk but never forgot how to play. The wrap bandage under Frank’s sleeve got thicker each October. The winces got harder to hide. The baseball — its signatures faded from thirty opening days — still flew the same arc. Until last Sunday, when it didn’t.

His arm stopped at his waist. Tried twice. Couldn’t rise. Frank held the ball in his calloused hand, looked at his son across the worn grass, and said the only words that mattered: "That might’ve been the last one, bud."

Terrence Boyd — fifty-two, shaved head, black security shirt, earpiece in, arms crossed — studied the stranger’s face the way he studied every face at the door: quickly, thoroughly, without giving anything away.
