Adrienne Walsh — the HR woman who'd called me — was already seated. Brunette. Pantsuit. The kind of professional blankness on her face that told me nothing.

He didn't look at me when I walked in. He was staring at the glass wall behind me, jaw tight, platinum watch catching the overhead lights. There was a third person I didn't expect.

A woman in a white lab coat with a name badge I couldn't read from the door. She had a small plastic case on the table in front of her — the kind that holds swabs and vials.

He still wasn't looking at me.

"Mr. Voss?" Adrienne said.

"Fine," he said. One word. Ice cold.

Forty-eight hours.

I barely slept.

Thursday morning. 6:45 AM. I was in the lobby before the lights were fully on. Took the elevator to thirty-seven. Walked into the conference room.

Adrienne. Dr. Ling. A man I didn't recognize — older, gray suit, legal pad in front of him. Probably counsel.

And Adrian.

He was already seated. But this time, he looked different. The controlled rage was gone. In its place was something I hadn't expected.

He looked scared.

Not the sharp, clean fear of a CEO facing a scandal. Something deeper. Something older. The fear of a child who discovers the ground beneath him isn't solid.

"The results are conclusive," she said, her voice clinical and calm. "The comparison shows identical genetic markers across all tested loci. The probability of this occurring between unrelated individuals is less than one in ten billion. The subjects are monozygotic twins."

Monozygotic. Identical. Same egg. Same cell division. Same everything.

He was staring at the folder. His platinum watch was trembling on his wrist again.

That story had just been cracked in half.

Because sitting across from him — in navy coveralls with a name patch, close-shaved head, calm hands — was the other half of that story. The version of him that didn't get the wealthy American parents. That didn't get the prep schools and the seed money and the last name that opened doors.

Because this wasn't a scandal. It wasn't a shakedown. It wasn't a liability to be managed.

It was two brothers. Separated at birth in 1986. Raised on opposite sides of the American experience. One became a CEO. One became a janitor. Same DNA. Same face. Completely different lives.

And now they were sitting twelve feet apart in a conference room, trying to figure out what to do with that.

Adrian didn't say yes to anything that day.

But he didn't say no either.

What he said — the last thing before he stood up and walked out — was this:

"Give me a week."

I nodded.

He paused at the door. Turned halfway. Not quite facing me. Not quite away.

"Marcus."

It was the first time he'd said my name.

"Yeah?"

"Don't... don't clock out early today. Your shift matters."

It was a strange thing to say. But I understood it. He was telling me he saw me. That my work — my six years of invisible labor in his building — wasn't nothing to him.

I nodded.

He left.

One week later — exactly seven days — I found a note in my locker. Handwritten on heavy cream stationery. No letterhead. No signature. Just an address and a time.

I showed up.

It was a diner. Not a restaurant. A diner. Vinyl booths. Coffee in ceramic mugs. The kind of place a janitor would choose, not a CEO.

He was already there. No suit. A gray sweater. No platinum watch. First time I'd ever seen him without it.

"I looked into it," he said when I sat down. "The orphanage. Leningrad. There's a woman in St. Petersburg who runs a records archive for adoptees. She found us. Marcus and Adrian Volkov. Twins. Born March 3, 1986. Separated at ten months."

Volkov. Our real last name.

"Neither do I."

"The board is going to have questions. The press will—"

"Adrian." I set my mug down. "I'm not your PR crisis. I'm your brother. We can figure out the rest later."

He looked at me for a long time. And for the first time — the absolute first time — I saw him smile. Not the CEO smile. Not the investor smile. A real one. Small. Uncertain. Like he'd forgotten how.

"Okay," he said.

"Okay."

We didn't hug. We didn't cry. We just sat in that diner booth and drank coffee and told each other about our lives — thirty-eight years of parallel existence, collapsing into one conversation at a time.

That was four months ago.

I still mop the floors. I told him I wouldn't quit — not yet. Because that job is who I am, and I'm not ready to become someone else just because my brother has money.

He comes down to the lobby sometimes now. Early mornings, when no one else is there. He brings two coffees. We stand by the windows and watch Detroit wake up.

Two identical men. Same face. Same blood. Different stories.

And we're writing a new one together.
