The names, the dates, the faces — those live in one kind of storage, and mine had been emptied out. So I had this strange, split existence. My mind looked at Sam and saw a kind stranger. My body looked at Sam and came home. I did not know how to tell anyone that without sounding as though my brain had broken in the middle, which I suppose it had.

I sat in my car in the church parking lot in Macon, Georgia, for eleven minutes with my two-year-old strapped in the back seat, telling myself all the reasons to drive home.

My name is Tamara Ellis. I am twenty-eight years old, and I am a single mother, and that Sunday I had come to audition for the choir at a church I had only attended twice, because I had heard they were forming a new ensemble and looking for voices.

The director's name was Glen Foss. Gray cardigan, reading glasses, the kind of man who has run the same program for thirty years and has very fixed ideas about how things are done.

I could feel his decision before I ever opened my mouth.

He looked at my denim jacket. He looked at my headwrap. He looked at the toddler I'd settled on a front pew with his crayons. And I watched him file me, quietly, under not quite right.

I have been waved off a lot of risers in my life. People decide what you are by what you're wearing and who's hanging off your hip, and they decide it fast, and they are very polite about it, and that politeness is its own kind of door slamming.

"Once she signs, the house is basically already sold. She won't even understand what she's signing."

That is what I heard my son say into his phone, in the hallway outside the day room, when he thought I was napping.

My name is Eleanor Frost. I am eighty-one years old. I have a bad hip and a good mind, and my son had gotten those two things confused.

I have lived at a pleasant assisted-living facility in Scottsdale, Arizona, for two years. It has big windows and too many cactus arrangements and a day room full of light. My late husband and I planned for this. We were careful people. There is a house, paid off, that we always intended to leave to help our grandchildren through school.

My son, Brett, is forty-nine. He wears a blazer over a golf polo and he has, let us say, a relationship with money that runs in only one direction.

For two years, Brett visited rarely. Birthdays, if I was lucky. He would breeze in, check his watch, and breeze out.

Then, three months ago, he started coming every week. Suddenly attentive. Suddenly worried about my "burden." He started saying things like, "Mom, managing the house and your accounts is just too much stress for you now," and "Wouldn't it be easier if I handled everything?"

He brought me a document. Power of attorney. He explained it the way you explain things to a child or a dog — slowly, warmly, watching to see if I'd swallow it. If I signed it, he said, he could "take care of all the boring paperwork" so I could "just relax and enjoy my days."

I am old. I am not finished.

I raised three children and ran a household budget through a recession and nursed my husband through his last two years, and somewhere in my son's mind all of that competence had quietly expired, like a coupon, the moment I needed a wheelchair to get to the dining room. He had decided that a bad hip meant a bad mind. People do that. They watch your body slow down and assume the lights have gone off upstairs, and they start talking about you in the third person while you are sitting right there.

I was sitting right there. I heard every word.

So I did not sign it that first day. I told him I wanted to "look it over," and I watched a flash of irritation cross his face before the warm mask slid back on.

And then, last Tuesday, I overheard the phone call in the hallway. Once she signs, the house is basically already sold. She won't even understand what she's signing.

He had a buyer. He had a plan to move me to a cheaper facility two counties away — away from my friends, away from the staff I trust — and pocket the difference, along with the house that was meant for his own children's education.

I sat in my wheelchair in that sunny day room and I felt something I had not felt in years. Not heartbreak, though that came later. Clarity.

Because here is what Brett, in all his blazered confidence, had never bothered to learn about his mother.

There is a young woman who works here named Rosa Delgado. A certified aide. Teal scrubs, hair in a bun. For two years, Rosa is the one who has actually been here. She is the one who notices when my hands are too stiff to open the milk. She is the one who wheeled me to the window during the dust storm so I wouldn't be scared alone. She remembers my husband's name. She asks about my grandchildren by theirs.

Rosa never once asked me for a thing.

The week before, when Brett started pushing the papers, I had quietly asked the front desk to help me arrange a visitor. Not family. An attorney. A woman named Ms. Okonkwo, who came on a Thursday while Brett was at his golf, and who sat with me for two hours, and who confirmed in writing that my mind was, in the legal phrase she used, "fully and unambiguously competent."

So on Tuesday, after the phone call, I asked Rosa to do me one favor. To make sure that the next time my son came with his papers, she was standing in the room.

He came on Saturday. Blazer, golf polo, that confident smile. He slid the stack of stapled papers across the activity table toward me, uncapped a pen, and said, "Okay, Mom. Let's get this handled. Sign right here and you can stop worrying about everything."

Rosa stepped up behind my chair.

I folded my hands over my clutch, and I looked at my son, and I did not pick up the pen.
