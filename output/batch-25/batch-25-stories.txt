The grace, I have decided, is not in punishing a man like that. It is in handing him a screen and a chair and saying: choose by the voice this time. He did. He keeps doing it. That is worth more than any apology — and he gave me the apology too.

Here is what I keep on my refrigerator.

It is the crayon drawing Eli made on the front pew that afternoon, the one he was bent over while a man in a gray cardigan was telling me I wasn't the right fit. It is a wobbly stick figure with an enormous open mouth and a bunch of colored lines coming out of it like fireworks.

I asked him later what it was.

"That's you, Mama," he said. "Singing."

He drew it before I sang. Before the scholarship, before Ruth, before Glen Foss took off his glasses. He drew his mother with her mouth open and light pouring out of her, because to a two-year-old it was simply obvious what I was, even sitting in a car in a parking lot too scared to go in.

I almost didn't walk through the doors.

Now I walk through them twice a week, and I sing the bridge with everything I have, and I make sure my son is always somewhere he can see that the woman who tucks him in at 3 a.m. is also the woman with the fireworks coming out of her mouth.

Both things are true. They were always both true.

Somebody just had to hear me first.
