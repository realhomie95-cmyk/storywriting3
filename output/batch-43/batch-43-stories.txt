No bucket.

"Tell them," I said. "Tell us what happened."

She paused.

"Room 417. Unknown male patient. Stroke. No visitors. I walked in and I recognized him immediately."

The church murmured.

She held up the card.

Vernon woke up Saturday morning. Eight hours before his own funeral.

He insisted on coming.

"I wanted to hear what y'all really thought of me," he said, that dry laugh rolling through the church like distant thunder. "Figured this was my only chance to attend my own eulogy without being dead."

The church laughed. That healing kind of laughter — the kind that comes after terror, after disbelief, after the body needs to release something too big to hold.

I looked at my brother. Alive. Thin and tired and leaning on a cane, but alive.

"You could have called," I said. "You could have let me know before I stood up there and—"

"I know," he said. Quieter now. Just for me. "But I needed to hear it, Curtis. I needed to know that what I did with my life mattered. Not from a card. Not from a plaque. From your voice. In this room. In front of everyone."

My chest hurt. Not the bad kind of hurt. The kind that means your heart is too full for the space it's been given.

"It mattered," I said. "Vernon. It mattered."

He nodded. Reached over and squeezed my hand with those grease-stained fingers that had fixed a thousand cars and never charged half the people who needed them.

Vernon shook his head. "Leave it. It's a good picture. Better than the one on my driver's license."

The church erupted again. And this time it didn't stop.

"I'm sorry I couldn't reach you sooner," she said.

"You reached him," I said. "That's what matters."

She smiled. Held out the card — the thank-you note Vernon left on her mother's windshield in 1994. Yellowed. Creased. The handwriting unmistakably Vernon's: She's good now. Pay it forward when you can.

"I paid it forward," Denise said.

"You did more than that," I told her. "You paid it back."

Vernon came into the kitchen then, leaning heavy on his cane but grinning.

"Curtis," he said. "You got one thing wrong in that eulogy."

"What?"

"My golf game. You said I broke ninety last summer. I broke eighty-five." He winked. "Get it right for the next funeral."

I laughed. I couldn't help it.

My brother. Sixty-eight years old. Back from the dead — or at least, back from an administrative error that felt like the same thing. Still cracking jokes. Still correcting me. Still standing.

Still here.
