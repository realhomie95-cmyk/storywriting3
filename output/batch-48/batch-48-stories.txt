I sat down on the other bed. The one with the duffel bag. I looked at my twin — the same face, the same jaw, the same everything except the missing finger and whatever was happening behind his eyes that I could not reach — and I said the truest thing I knew.

The Navy charged me with a misdemeanor for the ID. Diaz filed the report — she had to; there's no discretion at a military checkpoint. I don't know if Torres or the counselor or someone else in the chain spoke up. I stayed away because he told me to. I broke through because I had to. And somewhere in between those two truths — the respecting and the refusing — is the shape of what it means to love someone you cannot save.

You cannot save them. But you can show up at the gate with the wrong ID and all ten fingers and a fear so big it makes you stupid.
