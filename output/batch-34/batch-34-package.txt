"Take the paintings. Nobody wants them anyway."

That's what my brother Vernon said to me across the conference table at Whitmore & Associates on Bull Street in Savannah, Georgia, at 2:14 PM on a Wednesday afternoon.

My name is Nadine Osei. I'm thirty-seven years old. I teach art history at a small college outside Atlanta. I make $61,000 a year. I drive a twelve-year-old Honda. And three weeks ago, my father Arthur Osei died in his sleep at seventy-nine, alone in the house he'd lived in for forty years.

He was a mechanical engineer. Retired. Quiet man. Kept to himself after my mother passed in 2016. He had a workshop in the garage and an attic full of things none of us ever really looked at.

Paintings. Dozens of them. Stacked against the walls in mismatched frames. Some oil on canvas. Some watercolor under glass. Some so dusty you could barely see the images underneath.

Vernon and Dale never set foot in that attic.

I did. Every Thanksgiving. Every Christmas. I'd bring Daddy a plate and sit with him while he pointed at the canvases and told me stories about where he found them — estate sales, barn auctions, a storage unit he bought at a foreclosure in 1994. He never said any of them were valuable. He just said he loved them.

But Vernon and Dale? They loved the house. The two cars in the driveway. The brokerage accounts. The life insurance payout.

So when the estate attorney read the will — equal thirds of all assets, to be divided by mutual agreement — my brothers moved fast.

Vernon is forty-four. Real estate developer. Tailored gray suit. Gold cufflinks. The kind of man who measures his worth in square footage and never lets you forget it. Dale is forty-one. Car dealership. Expensive watch. He doesn't lead — he follows Vernon, always has.

They came to that conference room with a plan.

Vernon took the house — three bedrooms, quarter-acre lot, appraised at $410,000. Dale took the cars and the brokerage account — roughly $280,000 combined. The life insurance was already assigned to Vernon as eldest son per a beneficiary form Daddy signed in 2009.

That left me with the paintings.

Twenty-two framed canvases. No appraisal. No insurance rider. No mention in the will beyond "personal effects and household items to be divided equitably."

Vernon didn't even try to hide his satisfaction.

"They're worthless, Nadine," he said, leaning back in his leather chair. "Daddy bought them at flea markets. None of them are signed by anyone real. You can hang them in your apartment if you want, but don't expect anyone to pay you for them."

Dale laughed. Didn't even add anything. Just laughed.

The estate attorney — a white-haired man named Whitmore, same as the firm — looked at me over his reading glasses. He didn't say a word. But I saw his eyes flick to the stack of canvases, then back to me. Something in his expression told me to wait.

So I did what I always do.

I stayed quiet. I signed the distribution agreement. I loaded the paintings into the back of my Honda one by one, wrapped in moving blankets I brought myself. My brothers didn't help. They were already in Vernon's Mercedes pulling out of the lot before I finished the second trip.

That night, I set the paintings against the walls of my living room. Twenty-two frames. Some as small as a dinner plate. Some as large as a window.

I started cataloging them the way I'd catalog pieces for a museum exhibition. Photographs of front and back. Measurements. Frame materials. Canvas condition.

And on the fourteenth painting — a seascape in oil, maybe eighteen by twenty-four inches, in a plain dark wood frame — I turned it around and found something.

A small brass plate.

Screwed into the back of the frame with two tiny Phillips-head screws. Tarnished green at the edges. And on it, engraved in block capitals, a registration number.

I've spent twelve years teaching students how to authenticate paintings. I've written papers on provenance chains. I've sat in archives at the Smithsonian and the Met tracing ownership records for canvases that haven't been seen in decades.

And I recognized that numbering system.

My hands started shaking so badly I had to set the painting down.

He arrives Thursday.

Because they didn't just give me paintings.

The brass plate was there. Small. Tarnished. The registration number I'd already matched in the database.

For the next four hours, they worked. UV light. Infrared reflectography. Canvas weave analysis. Paint cross-section sample — a micro-sliver from the edge. "Nadine," he said. "This is a Winslow Homer."

Vernon's finger jabbed toward Whitmore. "You — you let this happen. You knew—"

"I did not know the paintings' value at the time of signing," Whitmore said evenly. "Nor did any party. The agreement was executed voluntarily. It is final."

"And the IP trust," I added.

Vernon looked at me. "What IP trust."

Vernon read it. Dale leaned over his shoulder.

The room was very, very quiet.

"Daddy knew," I said. "He kept records. He kept the paintings. He kept the patents. And he wrote a will that put them together — in the hands of whoever paid attention."

"And I'm donating canvas number seven — the harbor landscape — to the Telfair Museum downtown. In Daddy's name. Arthur Osei Gallery of American Art." I paused. "You're welcome to visit anytime."

The door closed behind me.

I drove home with the windows down and the Georgia air coming through warm and sweet. The twenty-one remaining paintings were still against my living room walls. Tomorrow I'd have them properly stored. Insured. Appraised one by one — because who knows what else my father tucked into those frames.

But tonight I just sat with them. In the quiet. The way he used to sit with me in the attic — pointing at the canvases, telling stories.

He always said the same thing when I asked why he kept them.

"Because someone should love them, Nadine. And you're the only one who does."

He was right.
