"Take your finger paintings, Margot. The rest of us will split what actually matters."

That's what my brother said to me while holding a silver candlestick like he'd just won the Super Bowl.

My name is Margot Kincaid. I'm thirty-four years old. I restore oil paintings for a living — mostly water-damaged estate pieces and forgotten museum backlog. I work in a two-room studio in downtown Savannah with a magnifying lamp, a set of Japanese brushes, and a patience that my family has always mistaken for weakness.

Last Saturday, we gathered at our grandmother's Victorian on East Gaston Street for the estate division. Grandma Lenore passed six weeks ago — ninety-one, sharp until the last day, lived in that house since 1962. The will was simple: everything in the house to be divided equally among her three grandchildren.

That's me, my brother Trent, and my sister Caroline.

Trent is forty-one. He sells commercial real estate in Atlanta, wears polo shirts tucked into khakis like it's a uniform, and has a gold signet ring he bought himself after his first big commission. He walked into Grandma's house that Saturday like he was appraising a property — not mourning a woman.

Caroline is thirty-eight. Married to a financial planner. Silk scarves. Coordinating handbags. She doesn't have opinions of her own — she has Trent's opinions delivered in a softer voice.

I was the youngest. The quiet one. The one who studied art history instead of business. The one who spent summers in Grandma's attic looking at her paintings while Trent played golf and Caroline planned her wedding.

The paintings.

Grandma had twelve oil paintings stored in the attic. Some small, some medium. None of them framed expensively. They'd been there as long as I could remember — leaning against the sloped wall under a linen sheet, gathering dust while the rest of the house filled with antiques and silver and crystal that Trent and Caroline fought over.

Nobody ever looked at those paintings. Not once.

Except me.

I used to sit cross-legged on the attic floor and study them as a kid. One in particular always held me — a small harbor scene. Ships at anchor. A golden sky. The brushwork was confident and layered in a way that felt older than the others. Deeper. There was a craquelure pattern on the surface — a specific web of tiny cracks that develops over centuries in certain pigment combinations. I noticed it when I was twelve and didn't know what it meant. I noticed it again at twenty-two during my first conservation seminar.

I never said anything. Because nobody ever asked.

So Saturday came. The estate attorney laid out the process — walk through the house, tag what you want, negotiate overlaps, sign the division agreement.

Trent claimed the silver service. The grandfather clock. The Chippendale dining set. Caroline claimed the crystal, the jewelry box, the Persian rug in the parlor.

I asked for the paintings.

The attic paintings. The twelve dusty canvases nobody had touched in decades.

Trent laughed so hard he grabbed the doorframe.

"You want the junk?" he said. He looked at Caroline. She pressed her lips together to keep from smiling.

"Those things aren't worth the canvas they're painted on," Trent said. "I had a buddy look at one years ago. Said it was decorative — the kind of thing you'd see at a garage sale for twenty bucks."

"I'd like them anyway," I said.

"Done." He slapped his hands together. "Margot gets the finger paintings. Caro and I will take everything that's actually worth something."

Caroline nodded. Smiled at me with what she probably thought was pity. "That's sweet, Margot. Grandma would have liked that."

I signed the division agreement. They signed it. We had it notarized that afternoon.

Then I went up to the attic.

The light was coming through the dormer window — that golden late-afternoon light that Savannah does better than anywhere. Dust motes floated in the beams. The linen sheet was half-draped over the canvases. I pulled it off carefully.

Twelve paintings. Eleven of them were exactly what Trent said — decorative, pleasant, worth maybe a few hundred dollars at auction.

But the twelfth.

The harbor scene.

I knelt on the worn wood floor and lifted it into the light. Turned it in my hands. The craquelure was there — that distinctive web I'd studied in conservation class. The pigment layers were consistent with pre-1850 technique. And in the lower left corner, partially obscured by decades of yellowed varnish, there was a mark I'd never been able to see clearly before.

I held it at an angle. Let the sun hit it.

My hands started shaking.

I knew that mark. Every art restorer in America knows that mark.

I set the painting down. Pulled out my phone. Took three photographs — the full canvas, the craquelure detail, and the corner mark. Then I sent them to one person: Dr. Elena Vasquez, senior authenticator at the American Conservation Institute.

"Margot. Where did you find this. Call me immediately."

I didn't call her. Not yet.

Neither of them looked at me.

Neither of them noticed the canvas under my arm.

I put it in my car. Drove home. Locked my studio door.

And sat there in the dark with my hands still shaking, because I'm almost certain — almost certain — that my grandmother had a lost Thomas Cole landscape hanging in her attic for sixty years.

And my brother just signed it over to me for nothing.

Dr. Elena Vasquez called me four times before I answered.

"It's real," I said. Not a question.

"Margot, if this is what I think it is — and based on those three images, I am almost certain — you are holding View from the Harbor at Catskill, 1836. Thomas Cole. Oil on canvas. I sat down. My legs just — stopped holding me.

And the mark — the corner mark I'd first noticed as a child — was Cole's monogram, partially obscured by a later varnish layer applied sometime in the 1970s. Probably by whoever stole it, trying to disguise the piece.

Agent Okoro told me the 1971 theft was an inside job. A maintenance worker at the Atherton Museum removed four paintings during a renovation closure. Three were recovered within a year. The fourth — View from the Harbor — vanished. It changed hands through private channels for decades before going dark entirely.

How it ended up in my grandmother's attic in Savannah, Georgia, nobody could say. But there was no evidence Grandma Lenore knew what she had. She probably bought it at an estate sale in the seventies — she used to collect cheap paintings at auctions. It would have looked like any other landscape to an untrained eye.

Just not to mine.

But here's the thing — and this is the part that surprised even me.

I didn't keep the painting.

The Atherton Museum lost View from the Harbor fifty-three years ago. It belonged in their collection. It belonged to the public. My grandmother would have wanted it returned — she was a schoolteacher, she believed in institutions and doing the right thing and not taking what isn't yours.

I donated the painting to the Atherton Museum. Full return. No conditions.

The museum's board offered a finder's fee — standard for recovered art. Fifteen percent of appraised value. $2.13 million.

I accepted.

With that money, I did three things.

First: I paid off my studio lease for ten years. No more month-to-month anxiety. No more wondering if I could afford the rent and the conservation supplies in the same month.

Second: I funded a scholarship at the Savannah College of Art and Design — the Lenore Kincaid Conservation Fellowship. Full tuition for one student per year studying art restoration. My grandmother's name. On something that matters.

Third: I bought the eleven remaining paintings from the attic back from myself — meaning I had them properly appraised, cleaned, and hung. They're worth maybe four thousand dollars combined. Decorative. Pleasant. Exactly what Trent said they were.

But I love them. They're Grandma's. They smell like the attic — like dust and linseed oil and the lavender sachets she tucked behind the sheet.

Trent hasn't spoken to me since the court ruling. Caroline sends a Christmas card — no note, just her name.

I'm fine with that.

Because last week I was in my studio, working on a water-damaged Winslow Homer from a private collection in Charleston, and my phone buzzed. A text from an unknown number.

It was Elena Vasquez.

"Margot. Are you sitting down? Because there's a painting at a estate sale in Beaufort that I need you to look at. I think it might be another one."

I set down my brush. Pulled on my jacket. Grabbed my magnifying loupe.

I learned something important in Grandma Lenore's attic: the quiet one sees what the loud ones miss. The patient one finds what the careless ones throw away.

And sometimes — just sometimes — the thing everyone laughs at turns out to be the most valuable thing in the room.

Trent should have looked at the paintings.

But then again — I'm glad he didn't.
