"Same twelve families. Every single year."

My name is Deepa Rao. I am thirty-eight years old. I am a biostatistician. I work for a research hospital in Tempe, Arizona. My entire career is built on one skill: looking at data and determining whether something that appears random actually is.

And for two years, the Westbrook Elementary aftercare lottery had felt wrong to me in a way I could not articulate.

The program has forty slots. The school has roughly two hundred families who apply each spring. The PTA runs a "random lottery" in April and sends out acceptance emails. Simple. Fair. Transparent.

Except the same families get in every year. And the same families — mostly working parents, mostly single moms, mostly families who cannot afford private aftercare — get waitlisted. Year after year.

After I overheard that conversation in the pickup line, I went home and I did what I do.

I pulled data.

The school publishes lottery results in the PTA newsletter — just names, acceptance or waitlist. I went back through five years of newsletters. I built a spreadsheet. I logged every family, every outcome, every year.

Then I ran a chi-squared test.

For those who don't know: a chi-squared test tells you whether an observed pattern differs significantly from what you'd expect by chance. If a lottery is truly random, every family should have roughly the same probability of selection each year, independent of previous outcomes.

The results came back in under a minute.

The probability of the observed distribution — the same cluster of families consistently selected, the same cluster consistently rejected — occurring through genuine random chance was less than one in ten thousand.

Less than one in ten thousand.

That is not a lottery. That is a list.

I ran it again. Different model, same result. I checked for confounding variables — enrollment date, sibling preference, special needs priority. None of them explained the pattern.

Something — or someone — was deciding who got in.
