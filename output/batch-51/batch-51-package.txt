My name is Caleb Ashford. I'm thirty-four years old, and until seventy-two hours ago, I was a director at Ashford Maritime Industries — the shipping company my grandfather built from a single tugboat in Charleston Harbor in 1961.

My shares — the ones my grandfather left me — were diluted from 22% to 6% in a single motion.

By the time I found out, the filing was already with the Secretary of State.

I called Lillian Mercer. She's the corporate counsel. Fifty-five, auburn hair pinned up, always looks like she's about to apologize for something. She picked up on the first ring.

Silence.

"Lillian."

"He told me you'd already agreed. I didn't find out until after the signatures were filed."

The toolbox.

I thought he was being dramatic. Grandpa was like that — old-school, cryptic, a man who kept secrets the way some people keep faith.

I never opened it.

Until Thursday.

One share.

One share. One veto. Irrevocable.

But my grandfather knew. He planned this the way he planned his shipping routes — accounting for weather, for betrayal, for the things people do when they think no one is watching.

I sat down in the chair at the head of the table. Not Randall's chair — the one at the opposite end. Closest to the window. Closest to the harbor.

My grandfather's chair.

I set the toolbox beside me on the floor. I wasn't going to keep it on display. Wasn't going to frame the certificate or tell the story at parties.

But I wasn't going to let it go, either.

That afternoon I called a company-wide meeting. Four hundred employees. Most of them had heard rumors about the restructuring. Some had already updated their resumes.

I told them the truth. I told them what happened and what would happen next. I told them nothing about their jobs was changing — except that the person in charge was going to be someone who'd worked the docks before he ever sat in a boardroom.

I kept the toolbox under my desk.

Some nights, when I'm working late and the harbor lights come on, I open the lid and look at the oil cloth and the empty space where the certificate used to sit.

And I think about my grandfather. A man who built a company from a single tugboat. Who kept a secret for decades. Who gave me a rusted box and said don't open it until they try.

He was right.

They tried.

And the toolbox was enough.

Three years ago, Everett disappeared.

Not slowly. Not like people who drift away. One Wednesday he was at my kitchen table eating pulled pork and watching my daughters do cartwheels in the yard. By Friday his apartment was empty. His phone disconnected. His truck found at a rest stop off I-16 with the keys on the seat.
